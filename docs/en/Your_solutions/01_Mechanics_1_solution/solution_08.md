To calculate centripetal acceleration, we need to determine how fast the Earth is spinning at the equator. Even though we feel like we are standing still, we are actually moving in a massive circle as the planet rotates.

### 1. Identify the Knowns
*   **Radius ($r$):** $6378 \text{ km} = 6,378,000 \text{ m}$
*   **Period ($T$):** The time for one full rotation is 24 hours.
    *   $24 \text{ hours} \times 3600 \text{ seconds/hour} = 86,400 \text{ s}$



---

### 2. Calculate Tangential Velocity ($v$)
First, we find the speed of a person at the equator by dividing the circumference of the Earth by the time it takes to complete one rotation:

$$v = \frac{2 \pi r}{T}$$
$$v = \frac{2 \times \pi \times 6,378,000 \text{ m}}{86,400 \text{ s}} \approx 463.83 \text{ m/s}$$

This means if you are standing on the equator, you are traveling at roughly **1,670 km/h** just by existing!

---

### 3. Calculate Centripetal Acceleration ($a_c$)
Now we use the centripetal acceleration formula:

$$a_c = \frac{v^2}{r}$$
$$a_c = \frac{(463.83 \text{ m/s})^2}{6,378,000 \text{ m}}$$
$$a_c \approx 0.0337 \text{ m/s}^2$$

### Summary of Results
| Parameter | Value |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Tangential Velocity** | $463.83 \text{ m/s}$ |
| **Centripetal Acceleration** | $0.0337 \text{ m/s}^2$ |

For context, this acceleration is only about **0.34%** of the acceleration due to gravity ($g \approx 9.8 \text{ m/s}^2$). This is why you don't feel like you're being "flung" off the Earth, though you do technically weigh slightly less at the equator than at the poles!
