To find the height after the second bounce, we need to track the energy of the ball as it interacts with the floor. 

Since mechanical energy in this context is primarily **gravitational potential energy** ($PE = mgh$), the height the ball reaches is directly proportional to the energy it retains.

---

### 1. Understanding the Energy Loss
If the ball loses **30%** of its energy, it retains **70%** (or $0.7$) of its energy after each bounce.

* **Initial Height ($h_0$):** $2.0 \text{ m}$
* **Energy Retention Factor ($r$):** $0.7$



---

### 2. Calculating Height After Each Bounce
Because potential energy ($mgh$) is linear with respect to height, we can apply the retention factor directly to the height:

**After the First Bounce ($h_1$):**
The ball rises to 70% of its initial height.
$$h_1 = h_0 \times 0.7$$
$$h_1 = 2.0 \text{ m} \times 0.7 = 1.4 \text{ m}$$

**After the Second Bounce ($h_2$):**
The ball loses another 30% of the energy it had after the first bounce, meaning it retains 70% of $h_1$.
$$h_2 = h_1 \times 0.7$$
$$h_2 = 1.4 \text{ m} \times 0.7 = 0.98 \text{ m}$$

---

### 3. Final Result
You can also calculate this in one step using the formula for multiple bounces:
$$h_n = h_0 \times r^n$$
$$h_2 = 2.0 \times (0.7)^2 = 2.0 \times 0.49 = 0.98 \text{ m}$$

**The tennis ball will rise to a height of 0.98 meters after the second bounce.**
