To solve these problems, we use the formula for the **period of a simple pendulum**:

$$T = 2\pi \sqrt{\frac{L}{g}}$$

Where:
* $T$ is the period (seconds).
* $L$ is the length of the pendulum (meters).
* $g$ is the acceleration due to gravity (approx. $9.8 \text{ m/s}^2$ on Earth).

---

## 1. Period on the Moon
We know the period on Earth ($T_E$) is 4 seconds. On the Moon, gravity ($g_M$) is $\frac{1}{6}$ of Earth's gravity ($g_E$). 

Since $T \propto \frac{1}{\sqrt{g}}$, when gravity decreases, the period increases. We can set up a ratio:

$$\frac{T_M}{T_E} = \frac{2\pi \sqrt{L/g_M}}{2\pi \sqrt{L/g_E}} = \sqrt{\frac{g_E}{g_M}}$$



**Calculation:**
1. Substitute $g_M = \frac{1}{6} g_E$:
   $$\frac{T_M}{4} = \sqrt{\frac{g_E}{\frac{1}{6}g_E}} = \sqrt{6}$$
2. Solve for $T_M$:
   $$T_M = 4 \times \sqrt{6} \approx 4 \times 2.45$$
   $$T_M \approx 9.80 \text{ s}$$

**The period on the Moon would be approximately 9.8 seconds.**

---

## 2. Required Length for a 1-Second Period on Earth
To find the length ($L$) required for a specific period ($T = 1 \text{ s}$), we rearrange the original formula to solve for $L$:

$$L = g \left( \frac{T}{2\pi} \right)^2$$

**Calculation:**
1. Plug in the values ($g = 9.8 \text{ m/s}^2$, $T = 1 \text{ s}$):
   $$L = 9.8 \left( \frac{1}{2\pi} \right)^2$$
2. Square the terms:
   $$L = 9.8 \left( \frac{1}{4\pi^2} \right)$$
   $$L \approx 9.8 \left( \frac{1}{39.48} \right)$$
3. Final result:
   $$L \approx 0.248 \text{ m}$$

**The required length is approximately 0.248 meters (or 24.8 cm).**
