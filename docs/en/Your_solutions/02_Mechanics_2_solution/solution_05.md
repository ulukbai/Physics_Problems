To find the final speed and determine if kinetic energy is conserved, we use the principle of **Conservation of Momentum**. 

---

### 1. Calculating the Final Speed
In an inelastic collision, the two objects stick together and move with a common final velocity ($v_f$). The total momentum before the jump must equal the total momentum after.

The formula for conservation of momentum is:
$$m_1v_1 + m_2v_2 = (m_1 + m_2)v_f$$

**Given Values:**
* **Runner mass ($m_1$):** 70 kg
* **Runner initial velocity ($v_1$):** 3 m/s
* **Cart mass ($m_2$):** 140 kg
* **Cart initial velocity ($v_2$):** 0 m/s (stationary)

**Step-by-Step Calculation:**
1.  **Initial Momentum:** $(70 \text{ kg} \times 3 \text{ m/s}) + (140 \text{ kg} \times 0 \text{ m/s}) = 210 \text{ kg}\cdot\text{m/s}$
2.  **Total Combined Mass:** $70 \text{ kg} + 140 \text{ kg} = 210 \text{ kg}$
3.  **Solve for $v_f$:**
    $$210 = 210 \times v_f$$
    $$v_f = 1 \text{ m/s}$$

**The final speed of the cart with the runner is 1 m/s.**

---

### 2. Is Kinetic Energy Conserved?
**No, kinetic energy is not conserved.** In nearly all real-world collisions—especially "perfectly inelastic" ones where objects stick together—some kinetic energy is transformed into other forms of energy.



**The Proof:**
* **Initial Kinetic Energy ($KE_i$):**
    $$\frac{1}{2} m_1 v_1^2 = \frac{1}{2} (70)(3^2) = \mathbf{315 \text{ J}}$$
* **Final Kinetic Energy ($KE_f$):**
    $$\frac{1}{2} (m_1 + m_2) v_f^2 = \frac{1}{2} (210)(1^2) = \mathbf{105 \text{ J}}$$

**Explanation:**
You started with **315 Joules** of energy and ended with only **105 Joules**. The "missing" **210 Joules** were converted into:
* **Thermal energy** (heat generated by friction and impact).
* **Acoustic energy** (the sound of the runner hitting the cart).
* **Work** done to slightly deform the materials during the impact.

While **momentum** is always conserved in a closed system, **kinetic energy** is only conserved in "elastic" collisions (like two ideal billiard balls hitting each other).
